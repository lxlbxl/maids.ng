Phase 01
Strategic Foundation
& City Selection
Pre-Build 02 / 07
Before a single page is built, you must know which cities to target and in what order. Building the wrong pages first wastes domain authority and dilutes the signal you need to rank. This phase prevents that.
1
Map every city you physically service — then tier them

List every city, town, or district your business can realistically respond to. Then assign a tier based on revenue potential and competitive landscape.

  • Tier 1 — Build First: Cities with high search volume, proximity to your base, existing customers, or completed jobs on record
  • Tier 2 — Build Second: Adjacent cities you serve regularly but have no page presence in
  • Tier 3 — Build Last: Outlier cities you can serve but have no job history or local knowledge for yet
2
Apply the "Can I Write Something Real?" filter before listing a city

For each city on your list, answer one question before it earns a slot: Can I write something about this city that could not be copy-pasted to every other city? If the answer is no, the city is Tier 3. Do not build its page yet.

  • Do you know at least 3 neighborhood or landmark names in that city?
  • Have you completed one or more jobs there you can reference (even vaguely)?
  • Do you know anything city-specific about housing age, weather, regulations, or demographics that changes how you serve customers there?
3
Understand why GBP alone cannot solve multi-city ranking

Your Google Business Profile is proximity-bound. GBP proximity to the searcher is one of the heaviest local ranking factors in play right now. If your office or home base is 40 minutes from the city you're targeting, GBP carries you so far and no further. The solution is not more GBP optimization — it is your website structure.

⚠ Critical Distinction

GBP gets you found near your home base. Your website gets you found everywhere else. The entire strategy in this SOP is built on that separation. Do not conflate the two channels or expect GBP service-area settings to replace properly built city pages.

Build pages for

Cities where you have job history, local knowledge, or demonstrable service activity — and can write genuinely unique content.

Defer pages for

Cities where the only differentiator is the city name itself. A thin page actively damages domain authority. No page beats a bad page.

Phase 02
Site Architecture
& URL Structure
Architecture 03 / 07
How you structure your URLs and internal links is as important as the content itself. The pyramid model below is used by enterprise SEO agencies for national brands. It works at exactly the same level for local service businesses.
The Pyramid Structure
HOMEPAGE
↓ links to ↓
CORE SERVICE PAGES  /drain-cleaning  /water-heater-repair
↓ links to ↓
CITY-SPECIFIC SERVICE PAGES  /drain-cleaning/concord
↓ passes authority upward ↓
Each city page links back to its parent service page + homepage
4
Build the correct URL pattern for every city page

City pages must live under their parent service page, not at the root level. The URL structure itself is a ranking signal — it tells Google what you do and where you do it simultaneously.

✓ CORRECT: yoursite.com/drain-cleaning/concord yoursite.com/water-heater-repair/walnut-creek yoursite.com/sewer-services/pleasant-hill   ✕ WRONG — kills hierarchy and link equity: yoursite.com/service-areas/concord yoursite.com/locations
5
Build bidirectional internal linking between all levels
  • Homepage → links to all core service pages (in nav and body copy)
  • Each core service page → links to all its city child pages (in a visible "Areas We Serve" section)
  • Each city page → links back to its parent service page AND to the homepage
  • City pages within the same service → cross-link to 2–3 nearby city siblings where natural ("We also serve Walnut Creek and Pleasant Hill")
6
Avoid common architecture mistakes that kill multi-city rankings
Mistake Why It Fails Correct Approach
/locations page Flat hierarchy; no topical signal per city Nest city pages under service pages
All cities at root level No parent service page to inherit authority from Always use /service/city pattern
No internal links to city pages Pages become orphaned; Google may not crawl them Link from service page + sitemap
One generic service page, no city pages Zero geographic targeting beyond home city Build service pages first, then city children

Phase 03
Keyword Research
Per City
Research 04 / 07
Every city has a distinct search behaviour. Demographics, housing stock, weather, local regulations, and income levels all shape what residents search for — and how they phrase it. Generic keyword research done at the brand level does not serve city pages.
7
Build a primary + secondary keyword set for each city

For each city page, identify one primary keyword and 3–5 supporting keywords before writing a single word of content. Use Google Search Console, Google's People Also Ask boxes, and a keyword tool (Ahrefs, Semrush, or free alternatives).

Example — Plumber, Concord CA: Primary:   plumber concord ca Support:   drain cleaning concord · emergency plumber concord            water heater repair concord · sewer line concord ca
8
Research what makes this city's customers ask different questions

This feeds directly into the city-specific FAQ you'll build in Phase 4. Before writing the page, investigate:

  • Housing age: Older neighborhoods = more pipe failures, more drain issues, different materials
  • Climate: Cold winters = burst pipes, boiler questions; coastal = corrosion concerns
  • Local regulations: Some cities have specific permit requirements for HVAC, sewer, or plumbing work
  • New construction vs. established suburbs: Different product questions and urgency levels
  • Income/demographics: Affects interest in premium upgrades, warranties, financing options
9
Map keywords to page elements before writing

Never write first and optimise after. Assign each keyword to a specific on-page element before opening your CMS.

Keyword Type Place On Page Usage Notes
Primary Keyword Title tag, H1, first 100 words, URL slug Use exactly once in H1; natural in body
Secondary Keywords H2/H3 headings, body paragraphs, FAQ questions Do not force; let them appear naturally
Nearby Cities / Areas Proximity paragraph, footer service area list Written as natural geography, not lists
Neighbourhood Names Body copy, proof paragraph, meta description Only use names you can actually reference
FAQ Questions FAQ schema section, H3 question headings Use exact natural language customers use
✓ Best Practice

Run a quick Google search for [your service] [city name] and note every "People Also Ask" question that appears. These are real questions from real searchers in that area — they belong in your city-specific FAQ verbatim, with genuine answers.

Phase 04
Page Content:
The Three Pillars
Content Build 05 / 07
Most businesses build the URL and stop there. Google does not rank URLs — it ranks pages. Here are the three non-negotiable content elements that separate a ranking city page from a placeholder.
Pillar One — Hyper-Local Proof Content
10
Write content that could only exist if you genuinely know that city

This is the single highest-impact section of the page. It must contain information that proves geographic familiarity — not because you inserted the city name, but because you reference specifics that only a local service provider would know.

  • Name 2–4 specific neighbourhoods or districts within the city ("We regularly serve homeowners in the Todos Santos area, Concord Highlands, and the streets around Monument Boulevard")
  • Reference at least one local landmark in a service context — not keyword stuffing, but as natural spatial orientation
  • Include at least one job reference if you have one: "We recently replaced a full sewer line for a client near Willow Pass Road." Specific. Credible. Unreplicable.
  • Mention anything city-specific that affects the service — soil type, pipe age of older neighborhoods, local water hardness, permit process
✕ The Mail Merge Killer

Never write: "We proudly serve [City Name] and surrounding areas." This is the most common and most penalised pattern on SAB city pages. Google is actively identifying templated geographic claims and de-prioritising pages built on them. Every sentence that could apply to any city on your list is a sentence that weakens this page.

Pillar Two — City-Specific FAQ (3–5 Questions)
11
Build a FAQ that is genuinely unique to this city

A plumber serving an older downtown neighborhood gets different questions than one serving new construction subdivisions. Write 3–5 questions that reflect the real concerns of this city's customers, not every city's customers.

  • Draw from your keyword research (People Also Ask), real customer conversations, and city-specific context (housing age, climate, regulations)
  • Use exact natural-language phrasing customers use — not formal rewrites
  • Answers should be 40–80 words: full enough to be useful, concise enough for rich result eligibility
  • Mark up with FAQPage schema so Google can render FAQ-style rich results in search
  • At least one question should name the city explicitly in the question text
Pillar Three — Natural Proximity Signals
12
Give Google a geographic anchor through natural spatial language

When Google evaluates a page from a business with no physical address in that city, it looks for proximity signals — natural references to local geography that establish spatial context. This is the replacement for the "hard location signal" that a street address would provide.

Write your content as if you're explaining to a new customer exactly where you operate: "We serve Concord and the surrounding areas, including Clayton, Concord Highlands, and the neighbourhoods near Monument Boulevard." Natural. Geographic. Anchored.

  • Name 2–3 neighbouring cities or towns organically in the body text
  • Include 1–2 ZIP codes naturally ("We cover the 94520 and 94521 zip codes")
  • Reference local landmarks, major roads, or districts as spatial orientation — not as keywords
  • The goal: every geographic reference should read as something a local business owner would naturally say, not something inserted by an SEO

Technical 06 / 07
Content quality wins rankings; technical signals protect them. Every city page must be correctly configured before it goes live. None of these elements are optional.
13

Element Formula / Example Max Length

URL Slug /[service-keyword]/[city-name] — lowercase, hyphenated No params
Image Alt Text "[Service] technician in [City] [State]" — be descriptive 125 chars
14
Implement FAQPage schema on every city page with a FAQ section

Use FAQPage structured data (JSON-LD format, placed in the <head> or just before </body>). Each FAQ answer should match the visible on-page answer exactly. Validate with Google's Rich Results Test before publishing.

15
Implement LocalBusiness schema with service area declaration

On the homepage and key service pages, use LocalBusiness schema with an areaServed property that lists all cities in your service area. On individual city pages, include serviceArea referencing that specific city. This reinforces the geographic signal at the structured data layer.

16
Submit each new city page via Search Console after publishing
  • Add the full URL to Google Search Console → URL Inspection → Request Indexing
  • Update your XML sitemap and resubmit it
  • Add a link to the new city page from an already-indexed parent page immediately on publish — do not let it sit as an orphan
  • Monitor impressions for that URL in Search Console within 2 weeks; if zero, check for crawl errors, indexing issues, or canonical conflicts
17
Core Web Vitals & page speed — don't let technical debt kill good content
  • LCP (Largest Contentful Paint) target: under 2.5 seconds. Compress all images; use WebP format
  • Do not load heavy scripts above the fold on city pages; defer non-critical JS
  • Avoid duplicate content: each city page must be unique — do not use canonical tags pointing to a generic page if you want the city page to rank independently

Phase 06
QA: The Net New
Local Data Test
Quality Gate 07 / 07
No city page goes live until it passes this test. The Net New Local Data Test is the quality gate that separates a ranking page from a thin page — and a thin page from no page at all. Run this review every single time.
The 7-Point Quality Gate
1. Neighbourhood specificity:  Does the page name at least 2–3 specific neighbourhoods, districts, or local areas within the city — not just the city name?
2. Landmark or road reference:  Is there at least one local landmark, major road, or geographic reference used in a natural service context?
3. Job or project proof:  Does the page reference at least one completed job, past customer interaction, or service outcome in or near this city?
4. City-specific FAQ:  Are the FAQ questions genuinely shaped by this city's demographics, housing type, climate, or regulations — not copy-pasted from another city's page?
5. Proximity signals present:  Does the content naturally reference adjacent cities, zip codes, or spatial geography that anchors this page to a real service area?
6. Mail-merge language absent:  Has the phrase "We proudly serve [City] and surrounding areas" (or any equivalent boilerplate) been eliminated from the page entirely?
7. The swap test:  Read the page and mentally swap the city name for a different city on your list. If the page still reads as accurate and plausible, it has failed. Rewrite until it doesn't.
✕ If The Page Fails Any Point

Do not publish. A thin city page actively damages your domain authority and signals to Google that you are manufacturing geography rather than demonstrating it. Return to Phase 04 and enrich the content. Publication is not the goal — ranking is.

After Publishing — Ongoing Maintenance
Month 1–3: Monitor

Track Search Console impressions and clicks weekly. Expect 4–12 weeks before consistent impressions. Zero impressions at week 3 = crawl or indexing issue; investigate immediately.

Month 3+: Enrich

Add new job references as you complete work in that city. Update FAQ questions based on real customer questions. Add seasonal content (e.g. "Pipe freeze risks in Concord winters").

The businesses that dominate multi-city rankings are not the ones that built the most pages — they're the ones that refused to publish a page that couldn't prove it belonged on the map.
